Background: Descriptive Findings

The Descriptive Groundwork

The descriptive analysis that grounds the project comes from the quantitative component of a regional needs assessment conducted for the Chicago Lawyers’ Committee for Civil Rights (CLC) — a legal nonprofit focused on voting rights — supporting their Midwest expansion across Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, Wisconsin, and Michigan. The analysis covered all 437 counties in the five states, pairing turnout data from the 2024 Presidential General Election with demographic estimates from the American Community Survey.

The findings here establish where turnout disparities are concentrated and what demographic patterns track with them. They directly informed the predictor selection for the predictive model that anchors this capstone — but description alone cannot explain why some counties perform above or below what their structural conditions predict. That gap is what the model and case studies take up.

State-Level Overview

Across all five states, the analysis found steady patterns of turnout disparities — specifically large differences between low- and high-turnout quintiles in education level, racial/ethnic composition, and internet access. The table below summarizes turnout ranges and within-state variation for each state.

Turnout and Demographic Patterns by State

For each state, the choropleth shows the county-level turnout distribution; the demographic gap chart below shows where low- and high-turnout counties differ most. Use the tabs to switch between states.

Voter turnout rate by county in Illinois, 2024 General Election. Turnout ranged from 48% to 78%.

Illinois has a fairly even distribution of turnout across the state. County-level turnout ranged from 48% to 78%, and counties in the highest quintile voted 15.5 percentage points more on average than those in the lowest — a substantial within-state gap. Still, the two lowest turnout counties, Lawrence (51%) and Brown (48%), sit 4% and 7% behind the next lowest county respectively, suggesting localized pockets of particularly low participation in the rural west-central and southern parts of the state.

Largest demographic gaps between high- and low-turnout counties in Illinois.

The largest demographic differences between low and high-turnout counties were racial disparities. High-turnout counties had an average of 9.5 percentage points more white residents, while low-turnout counties averaged 7 percentage points more Black residents. Educational attainment also varied: low-turnout counties had an average of 4.3 percentage points more residents with no high school diploma, and 2.5 percentage points more with just a high school degree. High-turnout counties had 3.7 percentage points more residents with a bachelor’s degree and 2.2 percentage points more with some college. Place of origin was also a notable gap — high-turnout counties had an average of 5 percentage points more residents born out of state, while low-turnout counties averaged 4.7 percentage points more residents born in state. Other noticeable differences include high-turnout counties having an average of 3.1 percentage points more households with internet, and low-turnout counties averaging 2.1 percentage points more residents with a disability.

Voter turnout rate by county in Indiana, 2024 General Election. Turnout ranged from 38.5% to 77.9%.

Indiana features a longer tail at the low end of the turnout distribution compared to the high-turnout counties. LaGrange County (39%) is over 10% lower than the second-lowest turnout county, and the state as a whole features a cluster of counties between the 54–60% range. County-level turnout varied from 38.5% to 77.9%, with the highest quintile averaging 16.8 percentage points more than the lowest — the widest within-state gap of all five states.

Largest demographic gaps between high- and low-turnout counties in Indiana.

The largest demographic gap between low and high-turnout counties was in educational attainment: low-turnout counties averaged 6.4 percentage points more residents with no high school education, while high-turnout counties averaged 3.9 percentage points more with a bachelor’s degree. Racial composition also differed substantially — low-turnout counties had 3.3 percentage points more Black residents on average, compared to high-turnout counties averaging 7.2 percentage points more white residents. Place of origin was also a prominent difference, with low-turnout counties averaging 6.1 percentage points more residents of in-state origin, while high-turnout counties averaged 8 percentage points more residents born out of state. Other notable differences include high-turnout counties averaging more households with computers and internet (2.5 and 2.8 percentage points higher, respectively), and low-turnout counties averaging 2.3 percentage points more residents with limited English proficiency and 2.1 percentage points more with a disability.

Voter turnout rate by county in Michigan, 2024 General Election. Turnout ranged from 57.7% to 93.2%.

Michigan has a high floor for low turnout, with only six counties below 65%. Two counties — Leelanau (93%) and Keweenaw (90%) — exceeded 90% turnout, and the distribution features a cluster of counties between 80–86%. Overall, turnout ranged from 57.7% to 93.2%, with the highest quintile averaging 18.7 percentage points above the lowest. Michigan ranked third nationally in eligible voter turnout in 2024, a result the Michigan Department of State attributed directly to expanded access policies passed under Proposal 2022-2.

Largest demographic gaps between high- and low-turnout counties in Michigan.

Educational attainment was the largest demographic gap between low and high-turnout counties, with low-turnout counties averaging 8.4 percentage points more residents with only a high school degree and 3.7 percentage points more with no high school degree. High-turnout counties averaged 7.2 percentage points more residents with a bachelor’s degree and 5.9 percentage points more with a graduate degree. Racial composition was also prevalent — high-turnout counties averaged 7.2 percentage points more white residents, while low-turnout counties averaged 4.5 percentage points more Black residents and 1.1 percentage points more American Indian/Alaska Native residents. High-turnout counties also had higher shares of households with internet (3.6 pp higher) and computers (2.5 pp higher).

Voter turnout rate by county in Ohio, 2024 General Election. Turnout ranged from 40.8% to 84.3%.

While most of Ohio sits in a mid-turnout range compared to national averages, it features extremes in variation — with Holmes County recording by far the lowest turnout, over 10 percentage points below the second lowest. Overall, turnout ranged from 40.8% to 84.3%, with the highest quintile averaging 17.6 percentage points above the lowest.

Largest demographic gaps between high- and low-turnout counties in Ohio.

Educational attainment was the largest demographic gap, with low-turnout counties averaging 7.3 percentage points more residents with no high school degree and 7.2 percentage points more with only a high school degree. High-turnout counties averaged 7.6 percentage points more residents with a bachelor’s degree, 4.2 percentage points more with a graduate degree, and 2.7 percentage points more with some college. Technology access was also a significant differentiator — high-turnout counties averaged 7.2 percentage points more households with internet and 5.3 percentage points more with a computer, a gap nearly as large as the education gap. Disability was an additional difference, with low-turnout counties averaging 4.3 percentage points more residents with a disability.

Voter turnout rate by county in Wisconsin, 2024 General Election. Turnout ranged from 53.6% to 87.5%.

Wisconsin features many counties clustered between 70–76%. There is only one county below 64% — Menominee County (54%) — and Waukesha County stands as something of an outlier at the top with 88%. County-level turnout ranged from 53.6% to 87.5%, with a difference of just 13.5 percentage points between the average of the lowest and highest quintile counties — suggesting a more compressed distribution and more modest differences among counties on average.

Largest demographic gaps between high- and low-turnout counties in Wisconsin.

The largest demographic difference between low and high-turnout counties was in education: low-turnout counties averaged 9 percentage points more residents with only a high school degree and 4 percentage points more with no high school degree, while high-turnout counties averaged 8.2 percentage points more residents with a bachelor’s degree and 4.6 percentage points more with a graduate degree. Household technology access was also a prevalent gap, with high-turnout counties averaging 4.9 percentage points more households with internet and 3.5 percentage points more with a computer. In terms of racial demographics, low-turnout counties averaged 6.2 percentage points more American Indian/Alaska Native residents and 1.8 percentage points more Black residents, while high-turnout counties averaged 8.2 percentage points more white residents.

What the Descriptive Analysis Found

Across all five states, educational attainment was the most consistently large demographic difference between low and high-turnout counties. For every state, low-turnout counties had a much higher proportion of residents with just a high school degree or no high school degree at all. Conversely, high-turnout counties in all states had much higher levels of residents with either a bachelor’s or a graduate degree.

While not as uniformly pronounced as education, racial composition was also a large differentiating factor between low and high-turnout counties — though patterns did vary. Generally, low-turnout counties had higher shares of Black residents, while high-turnout counties had more white residents. Additionally, some states, such as Wisconsin, had low-turnout counties that had a much higher proportion of Alaska Native/American Indian residents. Another outlier was Ohio, which had much less pronounced racial differences — where low-turnout counties had higher shares of white and Black residents and high-turnout counties averaged more Asian American residents.

Access to technology also appeared as a key differentiator across most states. Looking at Ohio in particular, high-turnout counties had much higher averages of households with internet and computers, even more of a pronounced difference than race/ethnicity.

The final consistent demographic differences were in disability status. While less pronounced than most of the previous trends, low-turnout counties had consistently higher rates of residents with disabilities. Additionally, in Wisconsin and Illinois in particular, and to a lesser extent Indiana and Michigan, low-turnout counties had much higher rates of residents born in-state, while high-turnout counties had higher rates of residents born out-of-state.

From Description to Prediction

The descriptive findings establish that education, race, internet access, and disability status are the strongest demographic correlates of county-level turnout variation across the Midwest. These findings directly informed the predictor selection for the model. The expanded model also accounts for state-level institutional context via state fixed effects, which adjust for the cross-state turnout differences seen in the choropleths above.

The predictive model takes the next step. After asking which demographics are associated with low turnout, the next question is which counties perform above or below what their demographics predict. From there, how counties’ actual turnout compared to their predicted turnout will be used to identify cases for qualitative investigation.
