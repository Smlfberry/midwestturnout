Who Votes — and Why Some Counties Beat the Odds

A predictive model of county-level voter turnout across five Midwest states reveals systematic patterns of under- and over-performance. Six counties — selected from the top, middle, and bottom of the residual distribution — anchor a mixed-methods investigation into the structural and civic factors that shape participation.

Research Design

What This Project Does

This project extends a descriptive needs assessment conducted for the Chicago Lawyers’ Committee for Civil Rights (CLC) as part of their Midwest regional expansion. The prior analysis documented demographic patterns associated with low voter turnout across five Midwestern states — finding consistent gaps by education, race, internet access, and disability status. This capstone takes the next step: moving from descriptive patterns to a predictive model that identifies counties performing above or below their structural expectations, then investigating why through qualitative case analysis.

County-level analysis offers a complementary lens to individual survey data — it captures the aggregate civic environment in which participation decisions are made, and surfaces variation that individual-level models can obscure.

This project builds two OLS regression models predicting county-level turnout rates in the 2024 general election across Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, Wisconsin, and Michigan. Model 1 uses five structural predictors drawn from the American Community Survey. Model 2 adds racial composition and lower-tail educational attainment as a theoretically motivated robustness check informed by the prior CLC descriptive findings. Residuals from these models identify counties that perform better or worse than their structural conditions predict.

Those residuals are the core analytic object. Counties with the largest positive residuals are “over-performers”: something beyond their demographics is driving participation. Counties with the largest negative residuals are “under-performers”: something is suppressing turnout that the model cannot explain. Counties closest to zero are the “as-expected” cases.

Why Residuals? Turnout rates alone can be misleading. A county with 65% turnout might be underperforming if its demographics predict 72%, while a county with 58% might be over-performing given its structural constraints. Residual analysis shifts the question from how much to relative to what.

Each point is a county. The dashed line marks perfect prediction (actual = predicted). Counties above the line over-perform their structural conditions; counties below under-perform. The six case-study counties are highlighted.

Data & Methods

Data Sources

ACS 5-Year CVAP Denominators State Election Commissions

Turnout rates are calculated using Citizen Voting Age Population (CVAP) denominators from ACS estimates, consistent with best practices in voting rights research. Using CVAP rather than total population or VAP ensures the denominator reflects the legally eligible electorate.

Structural predictors are drawn from the 2024 ACS 5-Year estimates:

Predictor ACS Table Rationale
% Bachelor’s degree or higher B15003 Civic engagement, information access
% No high school diploma B15003 Lower-tail educational disadvantage
% Households with internet access B28002 Voter registration, information environment
% Limited English proficiency B16004 Ballot access barriers
% With a disability B18101 Participation barriers, registration gaps
% Below poverty line B17001 Resource constraints on participation
% Black or African American B02001 Racial composition, suppression exposure

All data were retrieved using the tidycensus R package. County GEOID codes are used as the merge key across all datasets.
