Case Studies

Six Counties, Three Stories

The six case-study counties were drawn from Model 4 residuals and represent three distinct patterns relative to model predictions. Over-performers suggest something beyond demographics is mobilizing voters, such as civic infrastructure or political context. As-expected counties serve as baselines where structural conditions accurately predict turnout. Under-performers raise questions about possible voter suppression, disengagement, or other barriers not captured by the model.

In each tail, one county represents an extreme case and the second a more moderate outlier — ensuring the analysis is not limited to only the most extreme cases.

Actual vs. predicted turnout for all six case-study counties. Colored dot = observed turnout; gray dot = Model 4 prediction. Gap labels show the residual in percentage points.

▲  Over-Performers
▶ Jasper IL 77.7% 66.3% +11.4 pp While there is no clear differentiating factor for mobilization to account for Jasper’s overperformance, the county’s smaller population size, partisan cohesion, and local development coordination may help explain why turnout exceeded structural expectations.

Jasper County, Illinois, has a turnout rate of 77.7% compared to a predicted rate of 66.3%, a +11.4 percentage point gap — making it the single most overperforming county in the dataset. Despite its overperformance in turnout, no formal mobilizing institution stands out. A Republican-dominated county, there were no identifiable GOTV organizations and no documented voter-mobilization initiatives leading up to the 2024 presidential election.

With a population of approximately 9,300, Jasper County may reflect a small, socially embedded community that the literature often links to elevated turnout norms and social pressure. In small communities, voting is socially visible: neighbors know whether their neighbors voted and the perceived consequence of any individual vote is amplified by smaller electorates (Putnam, 2008;1 Gerber, Green & Larimer, 20082).

Jasper also has an interesting profile economically, being in a state of relative economic momentum. While it had modest economic growth during 2024 and an increased poverty rate, inflation-adjusted GDP grew approximately 29% between 2018 and 2022, dramatically outpacing Illinois’s 1% statewide growth rate over the same period. Local development organizations such as Jasper County Economic Development Inc. (JEDI), established in 2012, indicate a level of community coordination and planning in economic growth and local development. The strong Republican identification of Jasper may also contribute to higher participation in a county with a relatively cohesive local political environment.

▶ Martin IN 66.1% 57.4% +8.7 pp Martin’s overperformance in voter turnout appears to be less about what mobilizes voters than about who lives there. NSWC Crane’s federal workforce — 6,500 employees in a county of fewer than 10,000 — generates civic participation beyond what the county’s demographic profile alone would predict. This case illustrates how the presence of a major federal institution and employer can drive higher turnout through compositional mechanisms as opposed to clear mobilizational efforts.

Martin County, Indiana has a turnout rate of 66.1% compared to a predicted rate of 57.4%, a +8.7 percentage point gap.

Similar to Jasper, Martin County did not have any obvious voter mobilization organizations or efforts leading up to the 2024 presidential election. While the county’s overperformance does not appear to stem from formal voter outreach efforts, it may instead reflect a compositional effect tied to the local economy and workforce structure.

Here, the economic activity is much more pronounced than in Jasper. Martin County is home to The Naval Surface Warfare Center Crane Division (NSWC Crane), which employs roughly 6,500 federal employees and contractors, including over 3,000 STEM workers and approximately 1,000 engineers. This base has hired more than 1,300 personnel in the last three years alone. Additionally, the average wages in Martin County are approximately 6% above the national average — atypical for a rural county and reflective of Crane’s concentration of high-skill federal employment. Since 2024, Martin has seen noticeable increases in both median household income and median property value — despite a stable employment rate from 2023 to 2024.

In a county of fewer than 10,000 residents, this large federal workforce can drastically reshape who actually lives there. The model sees Martin as a rural Indiana county, despite the fact that the workforce composition resembles a federal research community situated in a rural setting. Research on public-sector employment suggests that government employees are generally more politically active and civically engaged than the broader population — especially in professional or technical occupations like the NSWC Crane (Cooper & Reggie, 2025).3 The model’s standard demographic predictors — education, internet access, poverty, age — do not capture the civic culture that federal professional employment may generate independently of those characteristics

Local development organizations such as the Martin County Alliance reinforce this picture of community stability, and rising property values (approximately 6% in 2023–2024) signal continued economic growth. Taken together, Crane’s workforce concentration and the surrounding civic infrastructure produce turnout levels that exceed what the county’s rural, standard demographic measures would otherwise predict.

◆  As-Expected
▶ Rock Island IL 61.1% 61% ≈0.1 pp Rock Island serves as a baseline and validation case for the model. Its near-zero residual suggests that the model adequately captures turnout dynamics in structurally and demographically diverse urban-edge counties. This is an important result given that much of the broader dataset is composed of smaller rural counties.

Rock Island County, Illinois, has a turnout rate of 61.1% compared to a predicted rate of 61.0% — essentially a zero residual. Here, Rock Island serves primarily as a baseline comparison case. So, why does the model predict turnout so accurately here?

Part of the Quad Cities metropolitan area along the Mississippi River, Rock Island is an urban-industrial county with approximately 145,000 residents. The county is demographically diverse, with substantial Latino and Black populations. It has a balanced economy, including manufacturing, logistics, healthcare, and the Rock Island Arsenal, a major federal Army installation. Unlike Martin County, however, the federal workforce does not dominate the county’s economic composition, instead serving as one part of a more balanced economy. Historically, the county has leaned Democratic, but has remained competitive in recent cycles.

Rock Island does not appear to contain unusual mobilizing or suppressive dynamics. Illinois has a moderately accessible voting environment, and there are no clearly identifiable civic or economic factors that would be expected to alter turnout beyond what the model captures. Instead, the county reflects a relatively balanced combination of industrial, urban, racial, and institutional characteristics that align with the model’s underlying predictors.

Overall, this makes Rock Island important because it does not stand out. Despite its relatively diverse and complex demographic and economic composition, Rock Island performed almost exactly as the model predicted, suggesting the model performs well in capturing turnout dynamics in counties that are neither overwhelmingly rural nor heavily metropolitan.

▶ Rusk WI 73.6% 73.6% ≈0.1 pp Rusk serves as a rural validation case. Its near-zero residual confirms that the model adequately captures turnout dynamics in typical rural counties without unusual mobilizing or suppressing factors. Paired with Rock Island, the two as-expected cases demonstrate the model’s consistency across both rural and urban-edge contexts.

Rusk County, Wisconsin, has a turnout rate of 73.6% compared to a predicted rate of 73.6% — a residual of essentially zero. Like Rock Island, the question for Rusk is why the model predicts it so well.

Rusk is a small, mainly rural county of approximately 14,000 residents in Wisconsin. It is overwhelmingly white, has lower median income levels than the state average, and is quite economically dependent on agriculture and light manufacturing. Rusk shows no unique local civic infrastructure or mobilization efforts.

Rusk’s near-zero residual indicates that the model captures rural counties well when neither unusual civic capacity nor large-scale federal employers reshape the county’s composition. It is a quiet baseline case — showing how the model’s structural predictions translate well to observed turnout when the surrounding conditions are typical for the region.

▼  Under-Performers
▶ Lawrence IL 50.9% 57.5% -6.6 pp Lawrence’s underperformance highlights the limits of structural predictors alone in explaining turnout variation. Despite several characteristics commonly associated with higher participation — including high homeownership, partisan alignment, and modest economic infrastructure — turnout falls substantially below expectations. The contrast with counties like Jasper suggests that additional local social, civic, or organizational dynamics not fully captured by the model may shape whether participation exceeds or falls below predicted levels.

Lawrence County, Illinois has a turnout rate of 50.9% compared to a predicted rate of 57.5%, a −6.6 percentage point underperformance. Despite low income and modest educational attainment, the county has a high homeownership rate, which is often associated with higher turnout, while its very low median property values often suggest low turnout. The county also operates one of only eight Foreign Trade Zones in Illinois, indicating some degree of institutional economic infrastructure.

Still, there is little evidence of local civic or mobilizing infrastructure that might be powering the higher participation. There were no documented GOTV organizations during the 2024 election, no prominent civic associations, and little evidence of organized voter mobilization. While Lawrence’s strongly Republican political environment could provide some partisan mobilization, there are no clear organizational or institutional factors connecting the county to higher turnout.

Although Lawrence shares some characteristics with counties like Jasper County — including a smaller population size, high homeownership, and a strongly Republican political environment — the county underperforms relative to expectations. The divergence between the two cases suggests that structural characteristics alone are insufficient to explain turnout variation and that unobserved local social or civic dynamics not reflected in available data or county websites may also shape voter participation in ways that are difficult to directly measure.

▶ Gogebic MI 70.3% 79.7% -9.4 pp Gogebic’s underperformance suggests that political realignment may outpace the rebuilding of local mobilization infrastructure. Historically, Gogebic had a strong union presence that helped structure political participation and Democratic Party engagement. As the county shifted Republican, the role of Unions appears to have weakened as well, while there is limited evidence of other equivalent institutions emerging in its place. The case highlights how turnout can fall below structural expectations during periods of organizational and political transition in formerly union-centered rural communities.

Gogebic County has a turnout rate of 70.3% compared to a predicted rate of 79.7%, a −9.4 percentage point gap.

What distinguishes Gogebic is its political and institutional trajectory. Historically, the county was part of a cluster of union-oriented, consistently Democratic counties in Michigan’s Upper Peninsula. However, like many similar rural areas across the Midwest, Gogebic has undergone substantial political realignment, shifting from a consistently Democratic county that supported Barack Obama in 2012 to supporting Donald Trump in the past three presidential elections.

The county also faces considerable economic strain. Of the 7,038 households in Gogebic County in 2023, 17% earned below the Federal Poverty Level, while an additional 33% were classified as ALICE households — earning above the poverty line but still unable to consistently afford basic necessities — leaving roughly half of county households below the ALICE Threshold. Voter turnout also declined by approximately 6.5 percentage points between 2020 and 2024.

One possible explanation is that this political transition coincided with the weakening of older organizational structures that historically supported voter participation. While unions once played an important role in mobilizing voters, their influence appears to have weakened over time during the party realignment. While partisan preferences shifted, there is limited visible evidence that equivalent civic or organizational structures emerged to replace the motivating role unions once played. Although some nonprofit infrastructure exists within the county, there are few clearly identifiable organizations occupying a broad voter-mobilization function.

Gogebic’s case suggests that political realignment and organizational decline can disrupt established patterns of civic participation. As partisan preferences change, the weakening of institutions that historically structured participation may contribute to county turnout falling below the model’s expectations — highlighting the importance of local organizational infrastructure beyond demographic and economic characteristics alone.
