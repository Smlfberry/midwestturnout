Synthesis

What Three Phases of Analysis Reveal

This project moved through three phases: a descriptive analysis of county-level turnout patterns across five Midwestern states, a predictive modeling phase that quantified the demographic and structural predictors of turnout, and a qualitative case study phase that investigated what the model cannot explain. Each phase answers a different question. Together, they build a layered account of why voter participation varies so sharply across Midwest counties — and why no single explanation is sufficient.

Descriptive analysis

Phase One

The Descriptive Picture: Where Gaps Exist

The CLC descriptive analysis established the basic landscape. Across all five states, county-level turnout in the 2024 presidential election varied dramatically — from below 40% in some Indiana counties to above 85% in parts of Wisconsin and Michigan. The within-state gaps were substantial in every state: the highest-quintile counties outpaced the lowest by roughly 14 to 19 percentage points depending on the state.

The demographic correlates of these gaps were consistent across states. Educational attainment was the strongest and most uniform differentiator: low-turnout counties had substantially higher shares of residents with no high school diploma and only a high school degree, while high-turnout counties had much higher shares of college and graduate degree holders. Racial composition was also a persistent gap — low-turnout counties consistently had higher shares of Black residents and, in Wisconsin, American Indian and Alaska Native residents. Internet and computer access, disability status, and place of origin rounded out a picture of overlapping structural disadvantage concentrated in low-turnout areas.

These patterns are descriptive, not causal. But they establish where the participation gaps are — and they motivated the predictors included in the modeling phase.

Phase Two

The Model: Quantifying Structural Predictions

The predictive modeling phase asked a more precise question: given a county’s demographic and structural profile, what turnout rate would we expect? Four nested OLS specifications were estimated, each adding predictors to the previous. Model 4, which was used to identify case studies, includes twelve predictors covering education, race and ethnicity, age structure, internet access, poverty, and disability, plus state fixed effects. It explains approximately 86% of the variation in county-level turnout (Adjusted R² = 0.855, RMSE = 3.19 pp).

The single largest fit improvement came from adding state fixed effects (Model 2 → Model 3: adjusted R² rose from 0.54 to 0.81). This confirms that state-level institutional context — registration rules, ballot access, early voting availability, voter ID requirements — absorbs a substantial share of the variance that demographics alone cannot explain. Adding age structure and expanded race variables in Model 4 yielded a further gain to 0.86, meaningful but smaller.

Among the individual predictors, the strongest effects in Model 4 were lower-tail education (a 10-point increase in % no high school diploma is associated with a −6.6 pp change in turnout), young adult population (% age 18–24: −6.5 pp per 10-point increase), and poverty (% below poverty line: −3.4 pp). Higher education was the strongest positive predictor (% bachelor’s or higher: +5.3 pp). Racial composition showed significant independent effects for % Black (−2.1 pp) and % American Indian/Alaska Native (−2.1 pp), while % Hispanic and % Limited English Proficiency attenuated to non-significance in the full model, likely because state context absorbs shared variance.

A well-balanced model produces residuals approximately normally distributed around zero, and Model 4’s distribution is close to that. The counties at the tails — those that extremely over- or underperform their demographic predictions — served as the basis for case study selection.

Phase Three

The Case Studies: What the Model Cannot See

Six counties were selected from the Model 4 residual distribution to investigate what drives the gaps the model cannot explain: two over-performers, two as-expected, and two under-performers. The qualitative research draws on secondary sources, such as local economic data, organizational documentation, county history, and local reporting.

Taken together, the case studies reveal less about what exact factors explain the turnout difference and more about what the model fundamentally cannot see. Two counties — Martin and Gogebic — have plausible mechanisms that are grounded in documented, local conditions: a federal workforce that reshapes who lives in the county and possibly motivates turnout, and a historical decline of union influence and mobilizing infrastructure during political realignment.

Two counties — Jasper and Lawrence — remain much more unexplained despite representing the extreme residuals in the dataset in either direction.

Jasper and Lawrence are both small, rural, Republican, and have high home ownership, making their divergence the most analytically intriguing result. The model treats them as structurally similar. One votes 11 percentage points above prediction; the other 6.6 below. The possibility of social embeddedness — where smaller communities generate norms of voting pressure through social visibility and the increased perceived consequence of individual votes — is a theoretically compelling explanation for Jasper. But if that mechanism were primarily about small community size and partisan cohesion, Lawrence should look similar — which it doesn’t. As such, this divergence suggests that social embeddedness alone is insufficient and that something else is at play in Jasper that is absent in Lawrence. One tentative explanation is the local civic and economic infrastructure: Jasper County Economic Development Inc. (JEDI), established in 2012, signals some degree of community coordination that may extend beyond economic development and influence broader civic engagement. Still, this remains speculative, and the available secondary sources are unable to confirm it.

The comparison between Martin and Rock Island offers a parallel point about what the model does and doesn’t capture in counties with federal installations. Martin’s federal workforce — which employs high-skill STEM and engineering roles at NSWC Crane — could generate higher civic participation than the county’s standard demographics would predict. Research suggests government and professional employees participate at higher rates than their demographic characteristics alone would predict, meaning the model’s demographic lens may underestimate Martin’s likely turnout. Rock Island also contains a major federal installation — the Rock Island Arsenal — but here the federal workforce serves as one part of a more diverse economy rather than dominating it. The model predicts Rock Island almost exactly, suggesting that when no single federal employer concentrates an unusually civically engaged workforce, standard demographic predictors perform well.

Over-Performer
Cohesion in small, rural communities may drive turnout above structural expectations
Jasper County, IL · +11.4 pp · rank 437 of 437

No identifiable GOTV organizations or formal mobilization infrastructure. Overperformance appears to reflect the dense informal civic environment the literature links to elevated rural turnout — social visibility of voting, normative pressure through community and religious networks, and the amplified perceived consequence of individual votes in small electorates. Partisan cohesion and GDP growth of roughly 29% between 2018 and 2022 reinforce a picture of high internal community coordination.

Over-Performer
Major federal employers can generate civic participation beyond what demographics predict
Martin County, IN · +8.7 pp · rank 434 of 437

Home to NSWC Crane, which employs roughly 6,500 federal workers and contractors in a county of fewer than 10,000 residents. The model sees a rural Indiana county; the workforce more closely resembles a federal research community. Research consistently finds that government and professional employees participate at higher rates than their demographic characteristics alone would predict — Martin’s overperformance appears to reflect this occupational civic culture rather than any active mobilization effort.

Under-Performer
Structural potential can go unactivated without local organizational infrastructure
Lawrence County, IL · −6.6 pp · rank 7 of 437

Shares several characteristics with high-turnout rural counties — high homeownership, strong Republican identification, small population — yet falls well below predicted turnout. The contrast with Jasper suggests that structural characteristics alone are insufficient. Something in the local civic environment that would channel latent potential into participation appears to be absent or inactive, though secondary sources do not clearly identify what.

Under-Performer
Political realignment may outpace rebuilding of mobilizing institutions
Gogebic County, MI · −9.4 pp · rank 3 of 437

Historically a union-organized, consistently Democratic county. As it shifted Republican over the past three election cycles, the union infrastructure that once structured political participation appears to have weakened without equivalent civic organizations emerging in its place. Roughly half of households fall below or near the poverty line, turnout declined approximately 6.5 percentage points between 2020 and 2024, and there is limited visible organizational infrastructure occupying the mobilization function unions once held.

Validation cases — Rock Island, IL and Rusk, WI both returned near-zero residuals. Rock Island’s result in a diverse urban-edge county confirms the model performs well beyond purely rural contexts. Rusk’s result in a structurally typical rural county confirms it performs cleanly when no unusual civic or compositional factors are present.

What This Means

The three phases point toward a consistent conclusion: demographic and structural variables are necessary but not sufficient to explain county-level turnout variation. The descriptive analysis showed where gaps exist. The model quantified the structural baseline. The case studies revealed that what separates counties at the tails is often something the model fundamentally cannot measure — the density and health of local civic infrastructure.

Structural conditions set the floor. Whether a county reaches its structural potential appears to depend heavily on local organizational and social factors the ACS cannot measure.

This has broader implications for how turnout inequality should be understood. Education, poverty, age structure, and state institutional environment help shape what is possible. Still, whether a county reaches that potential appears to depend on local organizational and social factors, such as major institutional anchors or the presence or absence of mobilization infrastructure. Gogebic and Lawrence both had structural conditions that should have produced higher turnout. Neither did. The difference lies in something survey data cannot capture.

For organizations working on voting rights and civic participation, this suggests that demographic targeting alone — focusing on counties with low structural capacity — may miss counties where structural potential exists but is underactivated. Identifying those counties requires exactly the kind of mixed-methods approach this project employed: quantitative baselines to locate the anomalies, qualitative research to try and explain them.

What This Analysis Cannot Claim

This analysis is observational and cross-sectional. The relationships identified between predictors and turnout are associations, not causal effects. Several specific limitations apply.

State fixed effects absorb unmeasured state-level variation. The model controls for state context but cannot separate the specific contribution of voting access laws from other state-level factors like political culture, party infrastructure, and historical patterns. The institutional access argument is supported by the pattern evidence but cannot be causally identified from these data.

Age structure is partially captured but imperfect. Model 4 includes % age 18–24 and % age 65+, which together address known age-turnout relationships. However, full median age or a more granular age distribution would likely improve predictions further, particularly in older rural counties.

The qualitative analysis relies on secondary sources. County narratives draw on publicly available documents, economic data, news archives, and organizational reports — not primary fieldwork or interviews. The explanatory factors identified are theoretically motivated hypotheses, not confirmed causal mechanisms.

Single election cycle. This analysis covers the 2024 presidential general election only. Whether the residual patterns identified reflect stable structural features or election-specific dynamics cannot be determined without panel data across multiple cycles.

Next Steps

Several directions would strengthen and extend this analysis:

  • Primary source qualitative research — interviews with local election officials, nonprofit staff, and community organizers in the six case counties would allow the hypotheses developed here to be tested more rigorously
  • Panel analysis across multiple election cycles to assess whether residual patterns are stable or election-specific
  • Civic infrastructure measurement — incorporating organizational density data (nonprofit registrations, union membership rates, religious organization counts) as predictors would allow the informal social capital hypothesis to be tested quantitatively
